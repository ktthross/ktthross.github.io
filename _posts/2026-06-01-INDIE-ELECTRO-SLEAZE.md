Sometime last year I was put onto the band <span style="font-family: 'Courier New', monospace; color: #e040fb; font-weight: bold; letter-spacing: 2px;">Bassvictim</span>. Specifically I listened to their first and only album `Forever` (they have three EPs and one album according to wikipedia. i don't know. They all sound like albums to me). Was immediately enamored with Maria Manow's vocal style and delivery backed by Ike Clateman's production. I explored their catalogue and was hooked -- `Forever` is not the high water mark, we are flooded.  I am not a music reviewer so I will abandon any pretext that I can describe their music with witty metaphors or deep cultural knowledge and say that you should listen to them!  What I am more intrigued by is how the music was categorized. See, after listening to <span style="font-family: 'Courier New', monospace; color: #e040fb; font-weight: bold; letter-spacing: 2px;">Bassvictim</span> I quickly discovered <span style="font-family: 'Impact', sans-serif; color: #00e5ff; text-transform: uppercase; letter-spacing: 4px;">Ear</span> and <span style="font-family: 'Georgia', serif; color: #ff4081; font-style: italic; font-weight: bold;">Ninajirachi</span>.  Both artists well worth your ears if you have some spare time.  I had found my way to the <span style="font-family: 'Arial Black', sans-serif; color: #69ff47; letter-spacing: 1px;">indie electro sleaze hyper pop revival</span>.

Let me first say that I am intrigued and a bit nervous from excitement to be at the beginning of such a precisely and eclectically named musical movement.  Forget about the quality music for a second, in five years I'll be able to tell casual acquaintances that I really got into `indie electro sleaze hyper pop revival` and I can't imagine anything more authentic or cool than that.

Most of this happened at the turn of the year and the beginning of 2026.  The moniker came back to me on an instagram post by BrooklynVegan from 2026/05/30 (or thereabouts) where they classify the bands as <span style="font-family: 'Arial Black', sans-serif; color: #69ff47; letter-spacing: 1px;">Indietronica</span>, <span style="font-family: 'Arial Black', sans-serif; color: #69ff47; letter-spacing: 1px;">Electroclash</span> and <span style="font-family: 'Arial Black', sans-serif; color: #69ff47; letter-spacing: 1px;">Indie Sleaze</span>.  In particular they contextualize this with the headline "All your favorite 2000s indie trends are back". Now this caught me off guard because it lines up with the alleged (not sure if there is clear evidence of this yet) 30 year cycle of nostalgia.

Do I really like this music or am I just engineered to respond to it because I am conditioned to <span style="font-family: 'Arial Black', sans-serif; color: #69ff47; letter-spacing: 1px;">indietronica electroclash sleaze</span> from the longing for my carefree and naive youth?  But this doesn't matter, to me at least -- I know I like the music! -- _cogito, ergo sum_, what matters is how the reference to the 2000s, the social construct of nostalgia, changes the discussion or my feelings around the art.

An appeal to history and nostalgia is to act as a legitimizing force. The labels applied to the music are legitimized because we can point to an earlier period of music and the labels applied at that point.  This in conjunction with the similarity in style and instrumentation makes the argument that this is a revival and further reinforces the idea that a 30 year cycle exists. As a test, you can ablate the arguments. Take away the appeal to nostalgia. Just make an argument that the styles are similar. My assertion that two bands are similar is more vulnerable to a challenge than an appeal to history.  There is a high amount of subjectivity and context that can be quickly dismissed unless there is a quantitative measure of similarity ... which you would need to agree to. The appeal to history however, that, for whatever reason in our brains, is ~real~.

There is a point to this somewhere. Maybe it's in the text waiting for an eagle eyed critical thinker to point it out to me. Maybe it's still in my brain. In either case, you should check out the `indietronica electroclash sleaze hyper pop revival`. It is and it will be amazing.

On a personal note, please do not interpret this as a criticism of BrooklynVegan or Andrew Sacher. I am fond of BrooklynVegan, and Andrew and their efforts to chronicle music culture and expose every artist they possibly can.  I find their reporting a wonderful source of new music and I hope they continue to do this as long as they are engaged by it! If there is something sinister going on I don't know what I don't know.
